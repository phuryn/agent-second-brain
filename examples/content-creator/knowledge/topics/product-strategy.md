## Core Positions

**Position 1:** Most "product strategy" problems are actually organizational design problems in disguise.
- Supporting evidence: In 3 separate consulting engagements, teams that couldn't prioritize their roadmaps had the same root cause — unclear decision rights. Who gets to say no? Who owns trade-offs between customer commitments and new development? When that's unclear, roadmaps become political artifacts, not strategic ones.
- Counter-argument: Bad prioritization frameworks do cause bad roadmaps — if you're using effort/impact with no consideration of strategic fit, your roadmap will be random regardless of org structure.
- Why I still hold it: The org design fix unlocks the framework fix. Teams with clear decision rights naturally develop better prioritization instincts because their decisions have real stakes.

**Position 2:** Annual planning cycles are primarily useful for budget allocation, not strategy.
- Supporting evidence: Every company I've worked with that runs "annual strategy" also re-plans quarterly. The annual plan becomes a starting point, not a constraint. The real strategy lives in what teams do when the annual plan is wrong (which it always is).
- Counter-argument: Annual planning creates necessary forcing functions — teams that never commit to annual goals tend to drift opportunistically.
- Why I still hold it: The forcing function value is real, but it's a budget and accountability mechanism, not a strategy mechanism. Conflating the two leads to rigid annual "strategies" that nobody updates when conditions change.

**Position 3:** The fastest way to improve a product team is to reduce the number of things they're accountable for.
- Supporting evidence: Anecdotal across 8+ teams. The teams I've seen transform fastest reduced their active initiatives from 12+ to 3-4 focused bets. The constraint forces quality decisions that abundance of work never does.
- Counter-argument: Reduction can mean cutting things that matter; constraint for its own sake is not strategy.
- Why I still hold it: The counter-argument applies to bad reduction (cutting randomly). Principled reduction — based on what's actually moving the needle — consistently reveals which initiatives weren't strategic bets but just accumulated work.

## Tensions & Open Questions

- If strategy is really an org design problem, what does the PM role actually own? This hasn't been resolved clearly.
- Position 2 (annual planning = budget, not strategy) conflicts with the common advice to "set a 3-year vision." Where does vision fit if not in the annual plan?
- The "reduce scope" position (Position 3) needs a mechanism post — why does constraint improve quality? The data is there; the mechanism isn't fully articulated.
