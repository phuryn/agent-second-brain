# LinkedIn Templates

Templates for the three most-used content types. Use to eliminate blank-page friction.

---

## Template: LI-001 — The Counterintuitive Lesson

**ID:** LI-001
**Name:** The Counterintuitive Lesson
**When to use:** When you have a finding that contradicts what most PMs are told, or something you believed for years that turned out to be wrong.
**Estimated length:** 700-900 words

**Structure:**
```
[Opener: State the counterintuitive thing directly. Don't bury it.]

[Most people believe X. Here's what I believed for Y years.]

[The moment / experience that changed it — specific, named, dated or contextualized]

[What's actually true — the counterintuitive finding, explained with mechanism]

[Why the conventional wisdom exists — steelman it]

[What to do instead — 3-4 specific actions, not vague advice]

[Close: What changes when you see it this way]
```

**Example:**
> The best product discovery isn't talking to customers.
>
> I spent two years running weekly user interviews. Built a spreadsheet. Tracked themes. Followed every best practice in the Jobs to Be Done playbook.
>
> My discovery was rigorous. My product decisions were still mostly wrong.
>
> The problem wasn't the interviews. It was what I was listening for...

**Notes:**
- The opener must be specific enough to be interesting but not so niche that only 5% of readers relate
- The mechanism is the key section — "what's actually true" without explanation is just a hot take
- Don't start with "I" — restructure the opener so the counterintuitive claim leads

## Template: LI-002 — The Numbered Insight List

**ID:** LI-002
**Name:** The Numbered Insight List
**When to use:** When you have 3-5 distinct, non-obvious observations on a topic. Each item must be able to stand alone.
**Estimated length:** 400-600 words

**Structure:**
```
[Opener: The unifying claim or question these items answer]

[N things I've learned about [X] after [Y] years / [Z] companies:]

1. [Insight — not a platitude. One claim, one sentence.]
   [2-3 sentences expanding it. Specific.]

2. [Insight]
   [Expansion]

3. [Insight]
   [Expansion]

[Optional 4th or 5th — only if genuinely distinct]

[Close: The meta-point — what these items have in common, or what changes when you know all of them]
```

**Example:**
> After helping 3 Series B companies rebuild their roadmap process, I kept seeing the same mistakes.
>
> 5 things that look like prioritization problems but aren't:
>
> 1. Saying yes to everything is a strategy problem, not a backlog problem.
>    If your roadmap is full, the question isn't which items to cut. It's why your organization can't say no to stakeholders.
>
> 2. Quarterly planning doesn't cause misalignment — unclear strategy does.
>    Teams that fight over quarterly priorities are fighting over what the company is for. Fix that first...

**Notes:**
- Minimum 3 items, maximum 5. More than 5 becomes a listicle.
- Each item needs its own insight, not just a variation on the same point
- The close is what separates this from a random list — it should reframe the reader's understanding of the topic

## Template: LI-003 — The Single Story

**ID:** LI-003
**Name:** The Single Story
**When to use:** When you have one specific experience that illustrates a broader principle. The story must be specific — named company, named moment, real numbers.
**Estimated length:** 500-800 words

**Structure:**
```
[Opener: The most interesting moment of the story — not the setup]

[Context: 2-3 sentences. What was the situation, who was involved]

[What happened: The story, told with specific detail. Not vague.]

[The turn: What changed, what was revealed, what you realized]

[The principle: What this story illustrates — stated clearly and directly]

[The application: How a reader can use this insight now]

[Close: Optional — what this changed about how you think/work]
```

**Example:**
> We were 3 days from launch when the CEO killed the feature.
>
> Not because of bugs. Not because of metrics. Because he'd had dinner with one enterprise prospect who didn't want it.
>
> That was my first lesson in the difference between stakeholder feedback and signal...

**Notes:**
- Start in the middle of the action, not at the beginning
- Real names require permission; use "a Series B company I worked with" if needed
- The principle must be generalizable — if it only applies to the exact situation in the story, it's not a principle
