# LinkedIn Hooks

Opening and closing patterns that have worked for this audience. Load when writing an opener or closer, or when the current hook isn't earning attention.

---

## Opening Hooks

### Pattern: The Specific Number
**Format:** Lead with a precise number that creates immediate context.
**Why it works:** Specificity signals that what follows is based on real experience, not theory. PMs respond to data.
**Risk:** The number must be surprising or meaningful — a boring number ("I've had 12 meetings") doesn't earn attention.

```
After reviewing 47 product roadmaps, I found the same problem in 43 of them.
It has nothing to do with prioritization frameworks.
```

---

### Pattern: The Named Failure
**Format:** State a specific failure — yours or a client's — with enough detail to make it feel real.
**Why it works:** Failure hooks are more credible than success hooks. They signal honesty and earned experience.
**Risk:** Failure must be specific and interesting — generic "I made mistakes early in my career" doesn't work.

```
We shipped the wrong feature for eight months.
Not because we didn't talk to users. We talked to users every week.
Here's what we were doing wrong.
```

---

### Pattern: The Contrarian Claim
**Format:** State something that sounds wrong. One sentence.
**Why it works:** Creates cognitive dissonance that compels the reader to continue.
**Risk:** If the claim isn't actually defensible, the post collapses. Never use this for pure provocation.

```
Discovery interviews are overrated.
```

```
The best product teams I've worked with didn't use OKRs.
```

---

### Pattern: The Precise Observation
**Format:** Name something specific that the target audience experiences but rarely sees articulated.
**Why it works:** Creates instant recognition ("yes, exactly") — the strongest hook for a niche audience.
**Risk:** Must be specific enough to be meaningful, general enough that more than 10 people relate.

```
The most dangerous meeting in product is the one where everyone nods.
No objections. No questions. Just alignment.
That's usually when you're about to ship something nobody actually wanted.
```

## Closing Hooks

### Pattern: The Single Action
**Format:** One specific thing the reader can do with the insight — this week, not "someday."
**Why it works:** Closes the loop between knowledge and behavior. Readers who feel equipped come back.
**Risk:** Action must actually follow from the content. Tacked-on actions feel hollow.

```
Next time you're in a roadmap review, ask this one question before any priority discussion:
"What would have to be true for us to not do this?"
If no one can answer, you're not ready to prioritize.
```

---

### Pattern: The Reframe
**Format:** Restate the core insight with new language that gives it more reach or resonance.
**Why it works:** Ends with a quotable moment — something the reader might share or remember.
**Risk:** Must be genuinely new — not just a summary of what was already said.

```
Roadmaps aren't plans. They're negotiations made visible.
Once you see that, the fights over priorities make a different kind of sense.
```

---

### Pattern: The Open Question
**Format:** End with a question the piece has earned — one the reader can now meaningfully engage with.
**Why it works:** Invites comment without asking for it explicitly. Natural conversation starter.
**Risk:** Question must be specific enough to answer — "what do you think?" is too open.

```
What's the highest-stakes decision you made based on user research that turned out to be wrong?
I'll share mine in the comments.
```

## What Doesn't Work on LinkedIn (for this audience)

**Opening patterns that underperform:**
- Starting with "In this post, I'll share..." — tells not shows; skippable
- Starting with a question ("Have you ever felt like...") — works for broad audiences, not skeptical PMs
- Motivational opener ("Success is about showing up every day") — wrong audience entirely

**Closing patterns that underperform:**
- "Follow me for more content like this" — reads as desperate; never use
- "Drop a like if you agree" — performs fine on engagement but doesn't match voice
- Summary close ("So in summary, the key takeaways are...") — redundant; the post already made the points
