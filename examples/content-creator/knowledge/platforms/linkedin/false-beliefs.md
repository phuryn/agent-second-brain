# LinkedIn False Beliefs

Evidence log of things that seem like they should hurt performance — but don't.

---

## Belief: Long posts get less reach than short posts

**Why people believe this:** Conventional wisdom is that attention spans are short, mobile-first means brief. LinkedIn "best practices" articles consistently recommend under 300 words.

**What the data shows:**
- 2026-01-08 "Why discovery interviews are overrated" (1,100 words): 28,000 impressions, 89 comments — top post of Jan
- 2026-01-22 "5 PM mistakes" (280 words): 4,200 impressions, 12 comments
- 2026-02-14 "The $2M mistake" (950 words): 31,000 impressions — top post of Feb
- 2026-03-05 "Use OKRs better" (250 words): 3,800 impressions, 8 comments
- Pattern consistent across 20+ posts: long-form averages 3-4x the impressions of short-form

**Verdict:** For this audience (senior PMs, operators), long-form consistently outperforms short-form on reach AND engagement. Short-form gets more comments-per-view, but long-form gets more total comments due to reach. The "short post = more reach" belief is true for general audiences, not niche expert audiences.

**Confidence:** High (20+ data points, consistent pattern)

---

## Belief: Posting more than 3x per week hurts reach

**Why people believe this:** Fear of "flooding" the feed. Advice from LinkedIn growth accounts that audience fatigue reduces engagement.

**What the data shows:**
- Feb 2026: posted 5x/week — average impressions 12,400 (vs. Jan 3x/week average of 9,800)
- No measurable drop in engagement rate when frequency increased
- Some individual posts in high-frequency weeks underperformed, but average held

**Verdict:** No evidence of reach suppression from 5x/week for this account and audience size. May apply differently at 50k+ followers. Current evidence says frequency doesn't hurt, but low-quality posts (regardless of frequency) consistently underperform.

**Confidence:** Medium (one month of data; need longer to confirm)

## Belief: Controversial posts damage professional reputation

**Why people believe this:** Fear that pushback = career risk. Conservative instinct for professional content.

**What the data shows:**
- 2026-01-08 "Why discovery interviews are overrated": received significant pushback in comments — also generated 12 inbound consulting inquiries that month (vs. 2 in December)
- 2026-02-20 "OKRs are often a symptom of a strategy problem": 40+ comments arguing the point — also generated 8 newsletter sign-ups from commenters who disagreed
- Pattern: the posts that generated the most pushback also generated the most inbound interest

**Verdict:** Controversy that is well-argued does not damage reputation for this audience — it signals confidence and domain expertise. The risk is controversy that is poorly argued or appears to be contrarianism without substance. "Edgy but right" consistently outperforms "safe but boring."

**Confidence:** Medium (positive pattern but N is small; could be confounded by post quality)
