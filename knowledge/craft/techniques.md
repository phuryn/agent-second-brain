# Writing Techniques

A reference for craft problems. Load this when editing, when stuck, or when output is technically correct but not working. Organized by the problem, not the technique.

---

## Problem: The piece has no tension

The reader has no reason to keep going. Everything feels flat.

**Technique — Stakes Injection:** Name what's at risk if the reader ignores this. Make it concrete. Not "this is important" but "if you don't do this, [specific bad outcome]."

**Technique — Contrast Framing:** Before stating your point, show the world without it. "Most people do X. Here's what happens when you do Y instead."

**Technique — Time Pressure:** Add a temporal element. Not "this is useful" but "this is useful right now, because [thing is changing/about to change]."

---

## Problem: The opening doesn't land

The first paragraph doesn't earn the second read.

**Technique — Scene Before Point:** Start with a specific moment or situation before stating the idea. Readers enter a scene more easily than an argument.

**Technique — The Counterintuitive Opener:** Lead with something that seems wrong, then resolve it. "The best piece of advice I ever received was terrible advice."

**Technique — Stakes First:** Open with the cost of the problem, not the description of the problem. Skip the setup; the reader wants to know why they should care.

**What not to do:** Don't open with a dictionary definition, a rhetorical question, or "In this article I will..." These signal low confidence and train readers to skim.

## Problem: The piece is too long but nothing feels cuttable

Everything seems necessary. The piece won't get shorter.

**Technique — Single Sentence Summary:** Write one sentence that captures everything the piece does. Then cut anything that doesn't serve that sentence.

**Technique — Archaeology Cut:** Read only the first sentence of each paragraph. If you can follow the logic reading just those sentences, most of the body is scaffolding that can be cut or compressed.

**Technique — The Brutal First Read:** Read only the last sentence of each paragraph. These should be the strongest sentences. If they're not, restructure paragraphs so the point lands at the end, not in the middle.

## Problem: The piece is too abstract

Ideas without grounding. Reader understands the point but doesn't believe it.

**Technique — The Minimum Viable Example:** Find one specific, real instance of the thing you're claiming. Named, dated, with a number if possible. One concrete example does more than three abstract elaborations.

**Technique — Show the Mechanism:** Don't just say X causes Y. Show how X causes Y — what are the steps, the chain of causation. Specificity about mechanism is more convincing than assertion.

**Technique — Name the Exception:** State the condition under which your claim doesn't hold. Counterintuitively, this makes the main claim more believable, not less.

---

## Problem: The ending falls flat

The piece ends but doesn't land. Feels like it just stops.

**Technique — Echo Opening:** Reference the scene, example, or question from the opener. Bring the reader back to where they started, now with new understanding.

**Technique — The Reframe Close:** State the core insight one more time, but differently — with a new lens or implication that wasn't visible at the beginning.

**Technique — The Invitation:** End with something the reader can do with the idea. A question they can now ask themselves, a test they can run, an action they can take.

**What not to do:** Don't summarize ("In conclusion, we've seen that..."). Don't add a new point in the last paragraph. Don't end with a question that has no answer.

---

## Problem: The voice sounds like everyone else

Generic, safe, could have been written by anyone.

**Technique — The Unpopular Opinion Search:** Ask — what do I actually believe about this that most people in this space would push back on? Write that sentence. Put it somewhere visible.

**Technique — Specificity Override:** Replace every general term with the most specific accurate version. Not "many companies" but "most Series B SaaS companies." Not "this approach can fail" but "this approach failed at [named example] because..."

**Technique — The Conversation Test:** Read a paragraph aloud as if explaining it to one specific person you respect. Does it sound different from the written version? Close the gap.

---

## Problem: The piece is good but not memorable

Technically correct, passes the gate, but won't stick.

**Technique — The Quotable Sentence:** Find or write one sentence in the piece that could stand alone. Could it be a tweet? A pull quote? If no sentence qualifies, the piece probably needs one.

**Technique — Unexpected Order:** Deliver information in a non-default order. Start with the conclusion. Bury the setup after the payoff. The unusual structure signals that the content is worth attention.

**Technique — Make the Implicit Explicit:** Find the thing the piece implies but never says. Say it. Often the most interesting sentence in a piece is the one the writer was afraid to write directly.
