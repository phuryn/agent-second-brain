# Voice Archetypes

Use this file when calibrating tone for a new context, or when output feels off but you can't name why. These nine archetypes are reference points, not boxes. Most effective voices blend 2-3 with one dominant.

---

## The Nine Archetypes

### 1. The Practitioner
**Core quality:** Earned authority. Speaks from having done the thing, not studied it.
**Signals:** "In my experience," specific numbers, named mistakes, unsentimental
**Risks:** Can feel dismissive of theory; can tip into war-story fatigue
**Best for:** How-to content, lessons-learned, post-mortems
**Blend with:** The Teacher (to add structure) or The Skeptic (to add edge)

---

### 2. The Teacher
**Core quality:** Clarity as a gift. Makes complex things accessible without dumbing them down.
**Signals:** Analogies, step-by-step, anticipating confusion, "here's why this matters"
**Risks:** Can feel condescending if the audience already knows; can over-explain
**Best for:** Explainers, onboarding, technical breakdowns for non-experts
**Blend with:** The Practitioner (to add credibility) or The Challenger (to add tension)

---

### 3. The Challenger
**Core quality:** Productive friction. Names what others won't, questions consensus.
**Signals:** "Most people think X, but...", direct disagreement, reframes the premise
**Risks:** Can feel contrarian for its own sake; alienates when wrong without receipts
**Best for:** Opinion pieces, hot takes, "everyone is wrong about X" content
**Blend with:** The Practitioner (to back claims) or The Strategist (to offer alternative)

---

### 4. The Strategist
**Core quality:** Sees the game others are playing. Operates at the level of systems, not tactics.
**Signals:** "The real question is...", trade-off framing, second-order effects, long time horizons
**Risks:** Can feel abstract and disconnected from immediate problems
**Best for:** Leadership content, market analysis, "what's actually happening" pieces
**Blend with:** The Practitioner (to ground it) or The Teacher (to make it actionable)

---

### 5. The Storyteller
**Core quality:** Makes ideas memorable through narrative. Readers feel before they think.
**Signals:** Scene-setting, character, tension and resolution, sensory detail
**Risks:** Story can overwhelm the point; can feel manipulative if the lesson is forced
**Best for:** Personal essays, brand narratives, making dry data emotionally resonant
**Blend with:** The Teacher (to extract the lesson) or the Practitioner (for earned narrative)

---

### 6. The Skeptic
**Core quality:** Intellectual honesty. Holds ideas up to light, doesn't accept premises uncritically.
**Signals:** "But wait...", steelmanning opposing views, naming uncertainty explicitly
**Risks:** Can feel wishy-washy ("on one hand, on the other"); can undermine conviction
**Best for:** Research analysis, nuanced takes, debunking content
**Blend with:** The Challenger (to add conviction) or the Strategist (to offer a framework)

---

### 7. The Curator
**Core quality:** Filters and synthesizes. Saves the audience time by finding what matters.
**Signals:** "The best X I've found," tight synthesis, opinionated selection, source attribution
**Risks:** Can feel like a listicle without curation rationale; curatorial taste must be trustworthy
**Best for:** Newsletters, roundups, resource lists, "best of" content
**Blend with:** The Teacher (to explain why each item matters) or The Skeptic (to cut ruthlessly)

---

### 8. The Peer
**Core quality:** Horizontal relationship. Talks with the audience, not at them.
**Signals:** "I've been thinking about this too," uncertainty shared openly, invites response
**Risks:** Can undermine authority; can feel like fishing for validation
**Best for:** Community-building content, early-stage audiences, vulnerable personal content
**Blend with:** The Practitioner (to add expertise) or the Challenger (to add direction)

---

### 9. The Architect
**Core quality:** System-building. Presents a framework the reader can use to think about a whole class of problems.
**Signals:** Named frameworks, 2x2 matrices, taxonomy creation, "here's how I think about this category"
**Risks:** Frameworks can feel arbitrary or over-structured; framework-first writing can bury the insight
**Best for:** Original methodology, framework posts, "how I think about X" content
**Blend with:** The Teacher (to make it actionable) or the Practitioner (to validate it with real cases)

---

## How to Use This Reference

**Diagnosing voice drift:** If output feels off, identify which archetype the draft accidentally landed in, and compare to the intended archetype. The gap usually explains the problem.

**Calibrating for a new context:** Identify which 1-2 archetypes fit the platform + audience + task. Note the dominant one. Use it as a lens when reviewing.

**Voice is not style:** Style is word choice and syntax. Voice is the relationship the piece assumes with the reader. You can change style while keeping voice, or keep style while voice drifts. Check both separately.
